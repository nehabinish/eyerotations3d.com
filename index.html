Eye Movements

Any rigid body can move freely in three-dimensional space using six degrees of freedom: three translational (along x, y, and z axes) and three rotational (about those axes). The eye is treated as such a rigid body.

Here, we focus on the rotational components of eye movements, assuming that translational motion is absent and the head remains stationary.

Decomposing Three-Dimensional Rotations

A traditional way to analyze 3D rotation is by decomposing it into three sequential rotations about orthogonal axes:

  • Roll (about x-axis): torsional eye movements
  • Pitch (about y-axis): upward or downward gaze
  • Yaw (about z-axis): sideways gaze

Rotation about each axis is represented by a matrix:

$$ R_3(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ R_2(\phi) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\phi & 0 & \sin\phi \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin\phi & 0 & \cos\phi \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ R_1(\psi) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos\psi & -\sin\psi \\ 0 & \sin\psi & \cos\psi \end{bmatrix} $$

A general rotation is then given by:

$$ R = R_3(\theta) R_2(\phi) R_1(\psi) $$

Due to the non-commutativity of matrix multiplication, the final orientation depends on the order of rotations. For example, [A] and [B] have different outcomes, while [A] and [C] can reach the same orientation using different axes.

Donder’s Law

For any head position and gaze direction, the eye assumes a unique orientation, independent of how it arrived there.

Implementing Donder’s law requires a consistent way to reach every eye orientation. Rotation matrices can be used, but multiple sequences may produce the same final state.

The Gimbal Arrangement

To address Donder’s Law, nested coordinate systems are used (Gimbals), typically aligned vertically and horizontally. These allow consistent decomposition into rotation sequences.

Fick’s System

Fick’s system uses yaw → pitch → roll:

$$ R_{Fick} = R_3(\theta_F) R_2(\phi_F) R_1(\psi_F) $$

Helmholtz System

Helmholtz prefers pitch → yaw → roll:

$$ R_{Helmholtz} = R_3(\phi_H) R_2(\theta_H) R_1(\psi_H) $$

Euler’s Fixed Axis Theorem

Any rotation of a rigid body around a fixed point is equivalent to a single rotation about some axis through that point.

This reduces 3D rotation to a single vector rotation, often represented as:

Quaternions

A quaternion q describes a rotation by an angle \( \theta \) about an axis \( \hat{n} \):

$$ q = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2)(n_x \hat{i} + n_y \hat{j} + n_z \hat{k}) $$

More detail is available at Ben Eater’s quaternion visualization.

Rotation Vectors

If the scalar part of the quaternion is discarded, we get the rotation vector:

$$ \vec{r} = \tan(\theta/2) \hat{n} $$

Listing’s Law

Listing’s Law is a refinement of Donder’s: any final eye orientation can be reached via a rotation about an axis within a specific plane — Listing’s plane, perpendicular to the primary gaze direction.

The primary position is typically straight ahead gaze. Under Listing’s law, all rotation vectors lie in a 2D plane, simplifying analysis.

A rotation vector satisfying Listing’s law is:

$$ \vec{r} = \tan(\theta/2) \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \cos\alpha \\ -\sin\alpha \end{bmatrix} $$

Simulating Eye Movements

The GitHub repository contains code to simulate and visualize eye movement vectors, validating both Donder’s and Listing’s laws.
